Frequently Asked Questions

The page features contextual information about Agony Columns, Victorian culture, and everything in between.

What are The Times?

The Times was the most popular newspaper in Victorian England but also the times our exhibition explores. The years between 1860 and 1910 are often considered the “golden age” of newspapers in England, but most publications were eclipsed by the popularity of The Times, whose circulation rose from 5000 in 1815 to a staggering 58,000 by 1855 (Winkworth 61). Today, it is possible to analyze these newspapers using computational tools thanks to databases making digitized versions of these papers accessible.

What were the “Agony Columns”?

For much of the 19th century, Victorian newspapers featured a column on the first page known as the "Personal Advertisements," or simply "the personals." These were contributions written by individuals rather than journalists or editors, and often included complaint ads of all kinds, from lost loves to missing pets. The agonizing voice of the contributors was what eventually gave these personal columns the name of "the agonies" or "the agony columns."

Why were some written cryptically?

There are many answers to this question, and our exhibition attempts to provide some answers to shed light on the phenomenon. As newspapers grew in popularity, other cultural changes were also affecting Victorian society. Urbanization and industrialization contributed to a rising sense of anonymity in large cities, such as London, leading to what Benedict Anderson has called a "community in anonymity" (qtd. In Rubery 55). As a result, more people relied on the growing popularity of mass media, specifically the Agony Columns, as a form of communication, often with people they had no prior relationship. Such communication, however, was public, featured prominently on the front page of popular newspapers. In order to conduct private correspondences, contributors used codes and ciphers to avoid revealing their conversations to the Victorian public.

Who wrote coded messages in the Agony Columns?

It is impossible to document precisely all the contributors and readers of 19th century agony columns. Our exhibition, however, focuses on three primary groups: tales of crime, stories of love, and narratives for fun. Criminals occasionally used the agony columns to communicate and schedule rendezvous locations and times. Others used the personals to find and develop relationships, and The Times columns have documented more than one runaway wife or husband, only to find them eloped or with their lovers at a later date. For readers, however, the Agony Columns were a source of tremendous entertainment, and many would spend hours reading about the dramatic stories unfolding before their eyes. Some even attempted to decipher these messages with varying degrees of success. Although it is difficult to know for sure, the concurrent rise of the Sensation Novel alongside the popularity of the Agony Columns may have led contributors to use the agonies as an outlet for creative writing, blurring fact and fiction and providing entertainment for the Victorian public, not unlike the crossword of today.

Who have been famous readers-interpreters of Agony Ads?

Throughout the 19th century, many were intrigued by the Agony Columns, but some were more fascinated than the rest.

E.J. Wilson (? –1879)
Claimed to have written over 400 personal column messages between 1851-1870, E.J. Wilson was probably the single most prolific Agony Column message writer of the Victorian era. Not much is known about his life, but from what records remain he appears to have been a well-read Customs official. Leaving London for Ennis, Country Clare, he taught languages at a local school and died in Liverpool in 1879 (Winkworth 62). Numbered among the pseudonyms that Wilson used and addressed are: "To the Equator", "Indigo Blue", "Alexis", "Rogue et Noire", "Battledore and Shuttlecock", and many others (Winkworth 63).

Ignatius Pollaky (1828–1918)
Today, of course, the most famous Victorian detective is the fictional Sherlock Holmes. But in the 19th century, deductive minds also existed, and these people scoured the newspapers for evidence and correspondence. Ignatius Pollaky was one of these. Pollaky was not only known for advertising his own business in the Agony Columns, but also for inserting mysterious notes and messages in the newspaper relating to his cases. As it appeared in The Times on 31 July 1868: "Monsignor – Many roads lead to Rome, but how to return? The was is crooked, even for – Pollaky."

Sir Charles Wheatstone (1802–1875) and Lyon Playfair (1818–1898)
Both prominent and well-known men in Victorian England, these two friends shared the hobby of decrypting coded messages that they found in The Times. They managed to decrypt some especially short and difficult ciphers, occasionally sending the cracked codes back to the newspaper in plain English. Later, both would become known for the Playfair Cipher, invented for secret communications via the telegraph and used in World War I and World War II. Their code-breaking occasionally revealed that not all ciphers contained dire and important messages, but were occasionally posted simply for the challenge and effect on the newspaper's readership.One coded message that appeared several times in 1852 read: "Tig tjvhw it tig jfhiiwola og tig psgvw – F.D.N." The cracked cipher appeared on 3 March 1852 as a response from Playfair, addressed to F.D.N.: "The Times is the Jefferies of the Press".

Any relation to Agony Aunts?

Yes! But their relation is perhaps not as close as the two names suggest. Though newspapers became both affordable and extremely popular in the mid-19th century, the earliest periodical publications date back to the 17th century. And ever since the birth of the newspaper, interactive exchanges between readers and columnists were not only encouraged but actively sought after. These interactive columns ultimately gave birth to today's Agony Aunts, who provide general advice through newspapers and magazines to readers who send in questions or requests. The 19th-century Agony Columns covered by our exhibition, while also interactive columns, can be distinguished from Agony Aunts due to their lack of focus on providing advice. However, the contents of both domains would often overlap, as many posted and complained about their love lives (or lack thereof), occasionally promoting themselves as potential wives or husbands to others. However, during the second half of the 19th century, more specialized women-oriented magazines and newspapers were established in response to the demand for practical advice and marriage opportunities. Though Agony Aunts bear some relation to their similarly named Agony Columns, specialized newspapers such as the Englishwoman's Domestic Magazine and the Matrimonial News, may be seen as more direct precursors of contemporary Agony Aunt columns.
