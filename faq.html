Frequently Asked Questions

The page features contextual information about Agony Columns, Victorian culture, and everything in between.

What are The Times?

The Times was the most popular newspaper in Victorian England but also the times our exhibition explores. The years between 1860 and 1910 are often considered the “golden age” of newspapers in England, but most publications were eclipsed by the popularity of The Times, whose circulation rose from 5000 in 1815 to a staggering 58,000 by 1855 (Winkworth 61). Today, it is possible to analyze these newspapers using computational tools thanks to databases making digitized versions of these papers accessible.

What were the “Agony Columns”?

For much of the 19th century, Victorian newspapers featured a column on the first page known as the "Personal Advertisements," or simply "the personals." These were contributions written by individuals rather than journalists or editors, and often included complaint ads of all kinds, from lost loves to missing pets. The agonizing voice of the contributors was what eventually gave these personal columns the name of "the agonies" or "the agony columns."

Why were some written cryptically?

There are many answers to this question, and our exhibition attempts to provide some answers to shed light on the phenomenon. As newspapers grew in popularity, other cultural changes were also affecting Victorian society. Urbanization and industrialization contributed to a rising sense of anonymity in large cities, such as London, leading to what Benedict Anderson has called a "community in anonymity" (qtd. In Rubery 55). As a result, more people relied on the growing popularity of mass media, specifically the Agony Columns, as a form of communication, often with people they had no prior relationship. Such communication, however, was public, featured prominently on the front page of popular newspapers. In order to conduct private correspondences, contributors used codes and ciphers to avoid revealing their conversations to the Victorian public.

Who wrote coded messages in the Agony Columns?

It is impossible to document precisely all the contributors and readers of 19th century agony columns. Our exhibition, however, focuses on three primary groups: tales of crime, stories of love, and narratives for fun. Criminals occasionally used the agony columns to communicate and schedule rendezvous locations and times. Others used the personals to find and develop relationships, and The Times columns have documented more than one runaway wife or husband, only to find them eloped or with their lovers at a later date. For readers, however, the Agony Columns were a source of tremendous entertainment, and many would spend hours reading about the dramatic stories unfolding before their eyes. Some even attempted to decipher these messages with varying degrees of success. Although it is difficult to know for sure, the concurrent rise of the Sensation Novel alongside the popularity of the Agony Columns may have led contributors to use the agonies as an outlet for creative writing, blurring fact and fiction and providing entertainment for the Victorian public, not unlike the crossword of today.
