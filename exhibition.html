Ciphers of The Times Exhibition


This exhibition is the result of two years of research of 19th-century Victorian newspapers. For much of the 19th century, these newspapers featured a column on the first page known as the Personals. These were contributions written by individuals rather than journalists or editors, and often included complaints of lost loves, missing pets, and other miseries. As a result, they became known as the Agony Columns.

But the Agony Columns were also much, much more. Throughout 19th-century England, people often used the Personals as a way to communicate with one another, often in code or cipher, indented for particular recipients. This form of cryptic communication not only allowed for many to participate in love affairs discouraged by family members, but also to partake in criminal activities, plan escapes, elope, and communicate privately over long distances.

They became a source of infinite fascination. For some, they provided breakfast conversation and dinner-time gossip. For others, they became a source of entertainment, allowing readers to engage in code breaking and puzzles over fifty years before the first crossword puzzle. Private investigators not unlike Sherlock Holmes would read, post, and follow up on notices from the Agony Columns, whereas potential grooms and brides would scoure the newspapers for potential spouses. Later, they became prime material for fiction, contributing to the development of genres such as the Newspaper Novels, Sensation Fiction, and Detective stories. The Agony Columns were the first available medium for personal mass communication in England, and, like today, were both a source of anxiety and delight to those who participated in them.

This exhibition contains two stories. One follows the development of the Agony Columns in the Times, the most popular newspaper of the era, and recounts the many tales, ciphers, and novels that the columns engendered. Though not many know of their existence, they are still a source of fascination , and this exhibition not only tries to understand why the Victorians found the Agony Columns captivating, but also how and why we discuss them today, from contemporary novels to movies, tv shows, and video games.The other story told in this exhibition is a narrative experience. It attempts to question the fascination of Agony Columns by offering a non-linear story, allowing users to experience the lure of the Personals for themselves.
